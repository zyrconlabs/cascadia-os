# Why I Built This

*By Andrey Popovich — Houston, Texas*

---

I was five or six years old the first time I took apart a telephone.

Not because I was told to. Not for school. Because I needed to understand how the sound got through the wire.

My parents were both in the military — we lived in Uzbekistan, then part of the USSR — and they had exactly the kind of household where taking apart something that worked and not being able to put it back together was going to be a problem. I learned quickly. I also learned that once you understand how something works, you can't un-know it. That feeling of seeing inside a system — the moment it goes from magic to mechanism — became the thing I chased for the rest of my life.

---

## Cold winters. Distant travelers. A larger world.

When I was still a boy, my family moved to Sergiev Posad, Russia. Harsh winters. Limited resources. The particular difficulty of a small town in the early post-Soviet years.

What Sergiev Posad had, strangely, was travelers. People passing through from Europe and the United States, carrying stories of different cities and different problems. I would listen to them and think: there are places where the problems are bigger, the machines are more complex, and the people building them need more help. That's where I want to be.

I studied aerospace engineering at Bauman Moscow State Technical University. One of the most rigorous technical institutions in Russia. I spent time at Russia's Space Control Center working on supercomputers, automation systems, and robotics. That experience shaped how I think about systems more than anything else in my education.

The lesson I took from the Space Control Center, the one I've applied to every project since: **the difference between a system that works in theory and a system that works at three in the morning when something unexpected happens is almost entirely about how you handle failure.** You design for failure. You plan for the moments when things don't go right. Everything else is just optimism.

---

## A backpack. $1,000. One-way.

At nineteen, I sold my guitar, my amplifier, my camera, and my computer. That gave me roughly a thousand dollars. I bought a plane ticket to the United States.

I did not speak English. I had no contacts. I had no plan that went further than "get there and figure it out."

The early years were genuinely hard. I remember soups that cost ninety-nine cents because that was the budget. Not being able to open a bank account, taking a bus or driver license that expires in a few months. Navigating apartment applications, a new language I was learning from context — from watching people, from making a lot of mistakes in public and trying to look like I meant to do that.

I built things to survive. A window-washing business. Online sales. Photography. A Photo Booth system for a friend — hardware and software together — that ended up in over 150 locations across the US and Canada, embroydery - tshirt printing in the garage. Every one of those businesses taught me something different. I wasn't building a career. I was learning how the world works from the inside of it. You learn how much resources and effort it takes for it to work. I tell people - we can build anything - depends on the resources, goal accuracy and timeline.

---

## The work that mattered.

The industrial world found me. Crown Lift Trucks. Raymond West. Toyota.  Conveyco Technologies. Fortune 500 clients — Amazon, AT&T, T-Mobile, LG Electronics, the US Navy. Over four hundred projects. More than a hundred million dollars in project value. I became an American.

Conveyor systems. Automation. Material handling. Robotics. Large warehouses being transformed from places where people do repetitive physical work into systems that are faster, safer, more consistent.

I loved this work. There is something deeply satisfying about walking into a space that is broken — behind schedule, over budget, not working the way anyone planned — and being the person who figures out why and fixes it. I led over a hundred automation projects for Amazon and many large installations. I had consistent results - on time, on budget, safe and completion as described. I got good at seeing the whole system clearly when everyone else was focused on their piece of it.

---

## The year everything fell apart. And what I built in the dark.

Then the hard part.

My marriage ended. Our family went through divorce. We had to sell the house, split apart, start again from scratch. Rebuild everything that had taken years to build — not just financially, but the shape of daily life, what home means, what stability means when you have a daughter who is watching you and depending on you to be steady even when you're not.

Then Covid hit. Everything locked down.

I was managing Amazon construction projects during the day — large-scale industrial work that kept running because the warehouses had to keep running. In the evenings and weekends, while my daughter slept, I built. Cryptocurrency mining computers and systems. Power supply. Cooling. Automated temperature monitoring and control. Moved prototypes from the apartment - got a small warehouse and continued to expand. I was designing those systems the same way I design everything: for the moment when something goes wrong at two in the morning and nobody is watching.

I made good money from those machines. Combined with my job, I was saving carefully, rebuilding. I was dreaming about buying a home again. A place where my daughter could grow up in a good neighborhood, a real place, not temporary.

Then the California housing market did what it did after Covid. Prices went up faster than I could save. Every good neighborhood I researched was out of reach. I spent months looking and not finding. That particular kind of frustration — working that hard, being that careful, and still watching the target move away from you — is one I don't fully have words for.

So I researched differently. I looked at the whole country. I looked at where engineering and technology and industrial work were actually growing, where good schools existed, where the cost of living made sense, where I could build something real.

I moved to Texas.

---

## Houston. Home.

I found something here I didn't expect to find.

Texas has a particular kind of person — I'd heard jokes about Texas hospitality before I arrived, and I thought they were jokes. They're not. The people I've met here are genuinely warm in a way that I hadn't experienced since leaving Uzbekistan, which sounds strange to say but is true. There's a directness and a generosity that I recognize from people who build things and know what it costs.

The industrial and engineering culture here is real. NASA. SpaceX. Tesla. HP. Chevron. Texas Instruments. And thousands of smaller companies and engineers and builders working on problems that matter. I found a community of people who are passionate about building a better future — for their kids, for their state, for the country. That energy is not something you can manufacture. It either exists in a place or it doesn't.

I bought a home. My daughters have a yard. Sometimes it's still lonely — that particular loneliness of starting over in your thirties, of a family that used to be whole and isn't anymore — but I'm surrounded by people who are building things, and that helps more than I expected. That feeling is motivation to build to grow and to help others using technology. The same as before - from my first not working PCB board, first PC with empty hard drive, soups for 99c, no place to call home, broken home - to a bright, beatiful future, with science, technologies and the most importantly passionate people, that can build almost anything that we can imagine.

---

## Early 2025. A laptop. An idea that wouldn't let go.

I was frustrated.

My phone had a personal assistant that couldn't remember what I told it yesterday. The AI platforms I had access to were powerful but locked — you couldn't run them the way you actually needed to, at the scale you needed to, for the workflows specific to your actual work. I was spending real hours every week managing flows that an intelligent system should handle. And I kept thinking about what it would actually mean to have an operator you could *trust* — not a chatbot that generates text, but something that does real work, asks when it's uncertain, and doesn't break when the connection drops.

I connected a Telegram bot to an AI model. It worked. I stayed up too late.

I built a server on my laptop. Moved to a VPS when the laptop wasn't enough. Hit hardware limits. Moved back to my personal computer since I had GPU on it. Learned about API pricing the hard way and started developing a hybrid approach that could work within realistic constraints.

I have two daughters. I have a demanding job. Development happened in the margins — early mornings, late nights, weekends I could carve out.

Through the summer and fall of 2025, I was rolling out models, testing, adjusting, getting results that were good but not stable. At the beginning of 2026, I ran the first fully successful experiment on my M1 MacBook. The durability layer, the supervision kernel, the approval gates, the multi-operator flow, the memory system — everything worked together the way I'd designed it to work.

I showed it to some friends. I showed it to some engineers.

Their reaction was what pushed me to put it on GitHub.

## Why it matters.

Elon Musk said: *"Engineering is the closest thing to magic that exists in the world."*

I agree with this completely. I've seen it in a telephone I took apart at age six. In a automation computer technologies at the Russian Space Control Center. In a conveyor system that transformed how a warehouse operated. In a mining server cooling itself at two in the morning while I slept. In a Telegram bot that drafts a business proposal, shows it to you, and waits — actually waits — until you say yes before sending it.

The magic isn't in the individual components. It's in how they work together. In the moment when a system does exactly what you intended it to do, without being told, because you designed it right.

What I'm building is for the person running a small business without a full operations team. For the engineer tired of AI that's impressive in demos and unreliable in production. For anyone who has thought: I need something that actually helps — that remembers, that asks before acting, that survives a crash and picks up where it left off.

The US has a real shortage of workers in a lot of industries. And it's a problem across the world does too. Autonomous operators that are safe and trustworthy — not "nobody's watching" autonomous, but "capable of acting correctly without constant supervision" autonomous — are going to matter more over the next decade than most people currently understand.

I want to build operators that give people more time with the people they love. That handle the things that drain your energy and don't require your judgment. That ask before they act, because I've managed enough large projects to know exactly what happens when powerful systems don't.

---

I started with a telephone I took apart in Uzbekistan.

I'm building something now that I hope makes a real difference — for engineers, for small businesses, for people in the US and across the world who need better tools and deserve to trust the tools they use.

From Houston, Texas, with love for engineering and for everyone who helped me get here.

— Andrey Popovich
